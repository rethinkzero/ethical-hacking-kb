# What is Ethical Hacking?

Ethical hacking is the practice of intentionally probing computer systems, networks, and applications to find security weaknesses—but with something that makes all the difference: explicit permission from the owner.

Think of it like this: a security company hires someone to test their bank vault by actually trying to break in. The tester uses the same tools and techniques as real criminals would—drills, lockpicks, whatever works. But here's the key difference: they have a signed contract giving them permission to try. When they find a weakness, they don't steal anything. They write a report explaining how they got in and how to fix it.

✓ Ethical Hacking

  • Written authorization signed by the system owner
  • Clearly defined scope—what you can and cannot test
  • Goal: Find weaknesses so they can be fixed
  • Results in a detailed report, not stolen data
  • Legal and professional

✗ Illegal Hacking

  • No permission—often no one knows you're there
  • No boundaries—the attacker goes wherever they can
  • Goal: Steal data, cause damage, or demand ransom
  • Results in stolen information, financial loss, and legal consequences
  • Criminal activity punishable by law

> Why Organizations Hire Ethical Hackers

Companies bring in ethical hackers because they understand a fundamental truth about security: you can't defend against what you don't know exists. An internal IT team knows their systems intimately, but they also have blind spots. They know how things are supposed to work. An ethical hacker approaches with fresh eyes and asks the dangerous questions:

  • Penetration Testing: Simulating real attacks to see what an actual criminal could access
  • Vulnerability Assessment: Systematically scanning for known weaknesses in software and configurations
  • Security Auditing: Reviewing policies, procedures, and technical controls against best practices
  • Red Team Exercises: Full-scale simulated attacks that test not just technology but also the human and process elements of security

⟡ The authorization distinction isn't just paperwork—it's the entire ethical foundation of this field. The same Nmap scan that's a crime at 2 AM on a network you don't own becomes professional security testing when you have a signed scope document and a scheduled testing window.
